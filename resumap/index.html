JOIN

After college, I joined AmeriCorps and volunteered for two years. My first year was at JOIN.

JOIN completely transformed what I knew about homelessness. Folks could come in and get clean clothes, housing assistance, and showers. Many came to have a place to weather the weather or access the internet or just exist for a bit. The bustling day space allowed me to connect with people in a nonjudgmental zone and listen to stories of hardship, love, and loss.

As the Immersion Coordinator, I planned and led multi-day immersions to help groups of students learn about homelessness in Portland and talk with people living on the streets. I got to be a bridge builder and connect of people who may not have connected otherwise. The best part was facilitating huge transformations and witnessing perspectives shifting. Nothing better than watching world-views get rocked.

Alaskan AIDS Assistance Association

My second AmeriCorps year brought me to Alaska.

At JOIN, I met a lot of people who were suffering from addiction. I yearned to learn more about it. So I chose to volunteer at the Alaskan AIDS Assistance Association and lead the Anchorage Syringe Exchange. People came to get clean needles and dispose of dirty ones. Alongside the exchange, I managed a food pantry and administered HIV and Hepatitis-C tests.

The tests included one-on-one risk assessments which were centered on finding plausible solutions to lower an individual's risk of self and community harm. For many, it was an incredible relief to open up to someone who didn't judge them and didn't know their friends. For me, it was an opportunity share space and stories and learn more about addiction, the complexities of addictions services in America, and what people are experiencing day in and day out in our communities.

Big Brothers Big Sisters of Alaska

AmeriCorps was over, but I couldn't just leave Alaska. The Land of the Midnight Sun still called my name. So, I hopped on a plane and moved to Juneau.

There are infinite factors that contribute to a person's adversity every story is unique. But one commonality rang true among nearly every person I met during my AmeriCorps years. Somewhere along the line, broken relationships contributed to their hardships. So I wanted to work in nonprofits centered on relationship-building. Big Brothers Big Sisters was my way of connecting with that issue.

I interviewed potential Bigs and Littles and matched them together based on commonalities and personality...I was a professional friend matcher. I then supported the pairs throughout the life of their matches. It's wonderful to be able to say I had a part in so many wholesome friendships. And I got paid to do it? In the most beautiful place ever? Swoon.

Washington State School for the Blind

After a few years in Alaska, it was time to bid fare well. I moved home and landed a gig at the Washington State School for the Blind. I was a substitute teacher, and residence life counselor. After school each day, the students would come back to their cottages where we would work on building life-skills to foster independence.

WSSB is a particularly wonderful place. It introduced me to a culture where blindness and visual impairment are the status quo, and introduced me to so many inspiring and incredible people.

Appalachian Trail

Summer 2016: school was out, the sun was shining, and my girlfriend was walking from Georgia to Maine. Heck yes, I had to join!

She thru-hiked 2,189.1 miles. The .1 matters. I managed to hobble out a measly 1,160-something. We lived immersed in the sounds of the woods. We would walk 10 miles by lunch time. 10 more after. One day, we walked 30 miles. Another we hiked 19, bagged 6 mountain peaks, ran the last 7.5 miles, and still made it to camp before sunset. I'm only kind of exagerating. We ate enough food to feed single a human being for several years. We embraced beautiful people into our "tramily."

My trail name was Frog King.
